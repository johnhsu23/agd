NAEP examines students' ability to understand and create music and visual arts

The 2016 NAEP arts assessment was given to eighth-graders across the nation. Approximately one-half of these students were assessed in music, while the other half were assessed in visual arts.

Students responded to and critiqued existing works of music and visual art and created their own original artwork. In addition, the arts assessment asked students about their opportunities to access and engage in music and visual arts both in and outside of school. There was also a questionnaire for school administrators that focused on resources and school demographics regarding music and visual arts.

Music portion of the assessment

Students' ability to respond to music was measured in various ways. Students were asked to analyze and describe aspects of music they heard, critique instrumental and vocal performances, and demonstrate their knowledge of standard musical notation and music's role in society. For example, after listening to a piece of music, students were asked to identify the music's region of origin and to describe a feature of the music that helped them to identify the part of the world that they selected. The average responding score for music was reported on a 0–300 scale. The assessment included one music creating question; therefore, only the responding process in music was reported for the 2016 arts assessment.

Visual arts portion of the assessment

The assessment measured students' ability to respond to art as well as their ability to create original artwork. Responding questions asked students to analyze and describe works of art and design. The average responding score for visual arts was reported on a 0–300 scale. Creating questions required students to design and create their own works of art. For example, students were asked to create a self-portrait that was scored for identifying detail, compositional elements, and use of a variety of provided arts materials. The average creating task score was reported separately from responding scores as the average percentage of the maximum possible score from 0 to 100.

More about the arts assessment

Highlights of what we learned about eighth–grade students
No significant change in 2016 average scale score
Students taking visual arts & music classes scored higher
Percent of students engaging with arts education outside school declined

Average scores for eighth–graders were not significantly different between 2016 and 2008. In 2016 the score gap between White and Hispanic students narrowed in both music and visual arts, while there were no significant changes in the score differences for other selected groups.

The average responding scores in 2016 for eighth–graders who reported taking courses in music and visual arts were higher than those who reported not taking such classes.

In general, lower percentages of students reported engaging in music or visual arts activities outside of school in 2016, while, the percentages of students who reported taking music and/or visual arts classes did not change significantly between 2016 and 2008.

KEY FINDINGS
  • White and Asian/Pacific Islander students outperformed their Black and Hispanic peers in both music and visual arts.
  • Students eligible for the National School Lunch Program (NSLP) scored lower in both subjects than those not eligible.
  • There were no significant differences in the percentage of students who reported taking a music class across selected race/ethnicity groups and for students eligible and not eligible for NSLP.
  • Higher percentages of White students reported taking visual arts classes than their Black and Hispanic peers.
